The Scam Playbook

Every phrase they use. Every trick they pull. Explained.

  1. Legitimate internships send rejection emails to most applicants. If you "just got selected" from nowhere — with no prior application — it's fabricated urgency. Scammers broadcast this to thousands via mass WhatsApp/email blasts.

    Real companies follow a structured hiring process: job listing → application → screening → interview → offer. The absence of any of these steps is a red flag.

  2. Skipping interviews is not a perk — it's how scammers avoid scrutiny. They don't interview you because they don't care about your skills. They only want your payment.

    Every credible internship, no matter how small the company, holds at least a brief call or form screening to verify the candidate is right for the role.

  3. Employers pay interns. Interns do not pay employers. Any fee framed as "registration", "security deposit", "processing", or "material cost" is the core mechanism of the scam.

    Once you pay, the scammer either disappears or strings you along requesting more payments for "certificates", "ID cards", or "advanced modules".

  4. A certificate generated via an online template has zero market value unless it comes from an NSDC-affiliated or recognized institution. Scammers use the promise of certificates to lure students who are under pressure to build their resume.

    Verify: Does the certificate show up in any employer verification portal? Is the issuing organization registered? Usually the answer is no.

  5. Most legitimate internships in India offer ₹5,000–₹15,000/month at mid-to-large companies, after a selection process. Scams promise top-of-market stipends to everyone, unconditionally.

    If the stipend sounds too good to be true alongside a payment demand, you are reading a scam script. Real stipends are delivered, not promised in advance as bait.

  6. Artificial scarcity and time pressure are classic dark-pattern manipulations designed to stop you from thinking critically. The "deadline" is fabricated and resets for every new target.

    Take time to research before paying anything. A genuine opportunity will still be there tomorrow. A scam "expires" precisely because they don't want you to think.

  7. Remote work is legitimate and common. The problem is when "work from home" is used in combination with other red flags (no interview, upfront fee) to seem appealing without any accountability.

    A genuine WFH internship will have an onboarding call, email communication from a company domain, defined tasks, and a real manager — not just a WhatsApp number.

  8. Scammers use generic flattery to make you feel singled out and special. In reality, the same message is sent to thousands of students scraped from Naukri, Internshala, or LinkedIn.

    Ask yourself: Did they mention anything specific about your profile? Can you verify their company on LinkedIn or the MCA registry? Is their email from a company domain or a free Gmail/Yahoo account?
